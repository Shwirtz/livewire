Simone Biles was diagnosed with ADHD as a child and has taken medication for it since. In September 2016, a Russian hacker group broke into the World Anti-Doping Agency database and leaked the confidential medical records of several U.S. Olympic athletes. The point was to imply they had cheated at the Rio Olympics. One of the records belonged to Simone Biles. It showed she had tested positive for methylphenidate, a stimulant used to treat ADHD.

Biles had a therapeutic use exemption. She had followed every rule. She hadn't cheated at anything.

She also hadn't told the world she had ADHD. That was private medical information, and now it wasn't private anymore.

Her response was two tweets posted three minutes apart.

No press conference. No long statement walking through the trauma of it. Posted publicly, and then she was done with the conversation.

The hackers were counting on shame. They didn't find any.

## What "diagnosed as a kid" actually looks like

The Winkler and Corcoran stories are about adults who spent decades not knowing what was wrong. Biles is different. She was diagnosed young, started medication young, and the medication helped her focus in school. The pattern the system usually catches (can't sit still, brain moves fast, trouble concentrating on things that don't grab her) was there. Her grandparents got her assessed. The assessment came back. She started treatment.

That is, on paper, the system working. And it still doesn't feel like a win, because the part no one talks about is what it costs a kid to grow up knowing their brain is categorized differently. Even with early diagnosis and appropriate treatment, the label follows you. Other kids find out. Teachers treat you differently. You learn very early that some information about yourself is safer kept quiet.

Biles kept it quiet. She had no particular reason to go public with it. She was the most decorated gymnast in American history, she had won four gold medals in Rio, and her ADHD was a medical detail between her and her doctor. Then someone decided to weaponize it.

## What her brain actually did in the gym

Biles has said, in a few different places, that even with ADHD she could be laser-focused in the gym. That sentence tends to get used as inspiration. It's more interesting than that.

The same brain that drifts in a classroom, the one that can't hold attention on a worksheet, that gets up when it's supposed to stay seated, that processes the whole room at once instead of just the thing in front of it. That brain, in a gymnastics gym doing something it actually wanted to do, locked in completely. The ADHD didn't disappear. The context changed.

This is the thing that parents of kids who "won't focus" already know but rarely hear confirmed out loud: the focus isn't missing. It's selective. A kid who can't sit through forty minutes of math can spend four hours straight in a game or a sport or a creative project they care about. The attention works. It just works on its own terms.

The word for this is hyperfocus, and it's not a compensation or a coping mechanism. It's the same wiring that makes sitting through a boring class feel impossible. The brain goes deep on what it finds rewarding and pulls away from what it doesn't. For Biles, gymnastics was rewarding enough that the focus that fled in school showed up in full every time she walked into the gym.

She went on to become the most decorated gymnast in world history, with 30 World Championship medals and 7 Olympic gold medals across two separate Olympic campaigns. She has moves named after her, multiple of them, including skills so difficult no other gymnast was willing to compete them first. The ADHD brain that couldn't sit still in school was doing double-double dismounts on the balance beam at the Olympics.

## Why the hackers miscalculated

The leak was designed to imply that Biles had an unfair advantage. The medication she was taking is a stimulant. Stimulants are on the WADA prohibited list. Therefore: cheating.

What the hackers understood about ADHD was approximately nothing. Methylphenidate doesn't give a person without ADHD a performance edge. What it does is bring a person with ADHD closer to baseline functioning, specifically the level of dopamine in the prefrontal cortex that most people start with. It levels the playing field. It doesn't tilt it.

The therapeutic use exemption exists specifically for this. You get evaluated by experienced clinicians. You document the diagnosis. You apply for the exemption. It gets reviewed. If it's legitimate, it gets approved. Biles did all of that. USA Gymnastics confirmed it. The International Gymnastics Federation confirmed it. Every governing body with jurisdiction over her competition confirmed it.

She had done nothing wrong. The hackers published her private medical records anyway and called it exposure.

Her response, the two sentences with no elaboration and no apology, said something important not just about Biles but about what it means to have grown up with a diagnosis rather than acquiring one later. There was no relief in her statement the way there is in Winkler's or in [Barbara Corcoran's story](/they-get-it-too), which you can read in this same pillar. No sense of finally having a name for the thing. She already had the name. She'd had it since childhood. The medication wasn't a revelation. It was Tuesday.

What she refused to do was treat it like a secret someone had exposed. It wasn't a secret. It was just private. Those are different things.

## What this means if you're raising a kid who takes medication

A lot of parents are nervous about the medication conversation. Not nervous that their kid needs it (that ship has sailed, the pediatrician has weighed in, the kid is doing better), but nervous about what other people will think. Whether to tell the school. Whether to tell the grandparents. Whether the kid should have to explain themselves.

Biles didn't get to choose when to have that conversation. It was made for her, publicly, by people who wanted to use it against her. And her answer was that there was nothing there to use.

The stigma around ADHD medication is almost entirely about shame. The medication is prescribed by a doctor. It treats a real condition. It works. The only thing left is the cultural story that says a kid who needs help with their brain chemistry is somehow lesser than a kid who doesn't.

Biles competed at two Olympics, won more medals than any gymnast in history, and took her medication every morning. The two things are not in tension. The second one made the first one possible.

That's worth saying out loud to your kid, not once but regularly, the way you'd say any other true thing. The way your brain works is just how your brain works. It's not the interesting part of who they are. Same as [Henry Winkler](/they-get-it-too/henry-winkler), who covered his for 31 years. The interesting part is what they do with it.
